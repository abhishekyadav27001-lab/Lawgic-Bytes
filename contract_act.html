The Lecture Hall: Case Studies

Learn the rules through history.

The Offer

Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Co.

1893 • England

"We will pay £100 to anyone who catches the flu after using our smoke ball as directed."

The Harvard Analysis:

A company argued their ad was just a "marketing puff." The Court ruled it was a General Offer to the whole world. Mrs. Carlill accepted the offer simply by performing the condition (using the ball).

Modern Example: Bug bounty programs by Google/Apple.

The Acceptance

Lalman Shukla v. Gauri Datt

1913 • Allahabad High Court

A servant found his master's missing nephew. He later found out the master had offered a reward of ₹501.

The Harvard Analysis:

The servant demanded the reward. The Court said No. You cannot accept an offer you do not know about. Acceptance requires absolute knowledge of the proposal.

Rule: Ignorance of an offer defeats acceptance.

The Intent

Balfour v. Balfour

1919 • England

A husband promised to pay his wife £30/month while she was sick. He stopped paying. She sued.

The Harvard Analysis:

The Court dismissed her claim. Why? Because domestic and social agreements lack the Intention to create legal relations. The law does not enter the domain of dinner dates and marital promises.

Modern Example: Promising to buy a friend a beer.

Case 01

The Accidental Amazon Price

Amazon glitches and lists a ₹1,00,000 MacBook for ₹100. You immediately click "Buy Now" and checkout. Amazon later cancels your order saying it was a pricing error. You sue Amazon for breach of contract.

Legal Logic: Displaying an item on a website is an "Invitation to Offer," not an Offer. When you click "Buy," YOU are making the offer to buy it for ₹100. Amazon has the right to reject your offer before they ship it. (Principle from Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain v. Boots Cash Chemists).

Case 02

The Lost Wallet

You lose your wallet. You put up posters saying "₹5,000 reward to anyone who returns it." Rahul finds your wallet, sees the poster, and returns it. You refuse to pay.

Legal Logic: This is a General Offer. Because Rahul knew about the poster (unlike in Lalman Shukla), returning the wallet constitutes valid Acceptance by performance. A binding contract was formed. Pay the man.

Professor's Module 02

The Illusion of
Consent.

"A signature is merely ink if the mind was manipulated. Today, we examine the legal safeguards that protect human free will."

§14

Consensus Ad Idem

Section 13: Meeting of the Minds

Before we ask if consent is free, we must ask if it even exists. Section 13 dictates that two people consent when they agree upon the same thing in the same sense.

The "Peerless" Example

You agree to buy cotton arriving on a ship named "Peerless." But unknown to both of us, there are TWO ships named Peerless. You meant the one arriving in October; I meant the one arriving in December. Result? No contract. Our minds never met.

Party A
Party B

Overlap = Consent

The 5 Traps of Free Consent

Section 14: Consent is NOT free if caused by these.

Rule: Traps 1-4 make it VOIDABLE (Victim can cancel). Trap 5 makes it VOID (Automatically dead).

Coercion

Section 15

Physical threat or force. Committing or threatening to commit any act forbidden by the Indian Penal Code to force a signature.

"Sign this property deed or I will shoot you."

Undue Influence

Section 16

Mental domination. Using a position of power (Doctor/Patient, Guru/Disciple) to obtain an unfair advantage over someone's will.

A doctor overcharging a desperate, dying patient.

Fraud

Section 17

The deliberate lie. Making a false statement intentionally, or actively concealing a defect to deceive the other party.

Selling a car with a dead engine, painting it to look new.

Misrepresentation

Section 18

The innocent lie. Making a false statement, but honestly believing it to be true. No intention to deceive, but it still misleads.

Selling a 45-acre farm, genuinely thinking it is 50 acres.

Mistake (Bilateral)

Section 20

When BOTH parties are mistaken about a fundamental fact essential to the agreement. This makes the contract completely VOID (dead from the start), not just voidable.

"A" agrees to buy "B's" horse. Unknown to both, the horse died the night before in the stable. The contract is void.

The Jurist's Library: Landmark Cases

Precedents that shaped the law.

Fraud (Sec 17)

Shri Krishan v. Kurukshetra Univ.

Is Silence Fraud?

The Facts: A student fell short of attendance but didn't mention it on his exam form. The University didn't check properly and let him sit for the exam. Later, they canceled his results, calling his silence "Fraud."

The Jurist's Analysis:

The Supreme Court ruled in favor of the student. Mere silence is not fraud. The candidate had no legal duty to point out his own shortage of attendance. The University authorities should have checked the forms. Caveat Emptor (Let the evaluator beware) applies here.

Coercion (Sec 15)

Chikkam Ammiraju v. Seshama

Madras High Court

The Facts: A Hindu man threatened to commit suicide unless his wife and son executed a property release deed in favor of his brother. Terrified, they signed. Later, they sued to cancel the deed.

The Jurist's Analysis:

The Court held that the threat of suicide amounts to Coercion. Although suicide itself wasn't punishable (as the person is dead), an attempt to commit suicide was forbidden by the Indian Penal Code. The consent was forced, making the contract voidable.

Moot Court Simulator II

Identify the vitiating factor.

Docket 03

The Devoted Disciple

A spiritual Guru tells his wealthy but highly superstitious disciple, "If you gift your entire 100-acre estate to my ashram, your soul will achieve guaranteed salvation." The disciple, completely devoted and fearing spiritual ruin, signs the deed. A year later, his heirs want the land back.

Legal Logic (Section 16): This is a classic case of Undue Influence. A Guru-Disciple relationship is a fiduciary one where the Guru holds real or apparent authority over the disciple's mind. The Guru used this dominant position to obtain an unfair advantage. The heirs can have the contract set aside. (Based on Mannu Singh v. Umadat Pande).

Docket 04

The Silent Seller

You go to buy a horse from Mr. X. The horse looks perfectly healthy, but it actually has a hidden mental disease. Mr. X knows this but says absolutely nothing. You don't ask any questions. You buy the horse, and it goes crazy the next day. You sue Mr. X for Fraud.

Legal Logic (Section 17): The general rule is "Mere silence as to facts likely to affect the willingness of a person to enter into a contract is not fraud." Unless you asked him directly ("Is the horse sane?") and he lied, or if he had a fiduciary duty to tell you, it's your responsibility to check. Caveat Emptor (Buyer Beware) protects the seller here.

Professor's Module 03

The Broken
Promise.

"Not all contracts reach a happy ending. Some are broken by choice, and others are destroyed by fate. Let us examine the law of Performance, Frustration, and Damages."

§73
Section 37

The Obligation

The fundamental rule of contract law: Parties must perform their promises. You cannot simply walk away because the deal is no longer profitable. If you made the promise, the law expects you to fulfill it.

Section 56

Doctrine of Frustration

What happens if performance becomes impossible or unlawful after the contract is made? The contract becomes VOID. The law does not compel anyone to do the impossible (e.g., an earthquake destroys the factory).

The Price of a Broken Word

Sections 73 & 74: The Law of Damages

Core Principle: Damages are for compensation, not for punishment.

Unliquidated Damages

Section 73

When the contract doesn't state a penalty amount in advance, the Court calculates the loss. The breaching party must pay compensation for any loss that naturally arose in the usual course of things.

The "Remoteness" Rule

You cannot claim damages for indirect, remote, or special losses unless the other party knew about them when making the contract.

Liquidated Damages

Section 74

When parties pre-agree on a specific penalty amount in the contract itself (e.g., "If you delay construction, pay ₹10,000 per day").

The "Reasonable" Rule

Even if the contract says the penalty is ₹1 Crore for a 1-day delay, the Indian Courts will only grant a reasonable compensation not exceeding the pre-agreed amount.

The Jurist's Library

The Precedents that shaped the law.

Frustration (Sec 56)

Taylor v. Caldwell

England (1863)

The Facts: Caldwell agreed to let Taylor use his music hall for four grand concerts. A few days before the first concert, the music hall accidentally burned down to the ground. Taylor sued Caldwell for breach of contract.

The Jurist's Analysis:

The Court ruled the contract was frustrated. The existence of the music hall was an implied condition essential for the contract. Since the destruction was not Caldwell's fault, the contract became impossible to perform and was discharged.

Damages (Sec 73)

Hadley v. Baxendale

England (1854)

The Facts: Hadley's mill stopped working due to a broken crankshaft. He hired Baxendale (a courier) to deliver it for repairs. Baxendale delayed the delivery by several days. Hadley sued for the massive lost profits during those days.

The Jurist's Analysis:

The Court established the legendary rule of Remoteness of Damages. Baxendale was not liable for the lost profits. Why? Because he was just a courier; he didn't know the entire mill would be shut down without that one shaft. You only pay for losses that are reasonably foreseeable.

Docket 05

The Strike vs. Act of God

A shipping company agrees to deliver your cargo in 5 days. Suddenly, their own workers go on strike for better wages, making delivery impossible. The company claims the contract is "Frustrated" (Section 56) and they are excused from liability.

Legal Logic: Section 56 requires an event outside the control of the parties (Act of God, war, law change). A labor strike by your own employees is considered a commercial hardship or management failure, not a legal impossibility. It is a Breach of Contract, not Frustration.

Docket 06

The Catering Disaster

You hire a caterer for your wedding for ₹5 Lakhs. The contract says, "Penalty for failing to show up: ₹50 Lakhs." The caterer doesn't show up. You have to hire a last-minute replacement for ₹7 Lakhs (a loss of ₹2 Lakhs). You sue the original caterer for the full ₹50 Lakhs penalty.

Legal Logic (Section 74): Indian law does not enforce absurd penalties. A pre-agreed sum is just the "upper limit." The Court will only award reasonable compensation for actual loss proved. Since your actual loss was ₹2 Lakhs, you get ₹2 Lakhs. The ₹50 Lakhs clause is an unenforceable penalty.

Professor's Module 04

The Forbidden &
The Accidental.

"The law refuses to enforce certain promises because they harm society. Conversely, the law will invent a contract out of thin air to prevent one man from unjustly enriching himself at another's expense."

§27

The Forbidden Contracts

Agreements expressly declared VOID by Law

Section 26

Restraint of Marriage

You cannot pay someone to never marry, or to not marry a specific person. The right to marry is a fundamental human liberty. Any contract restricting this (except for minors) is dead on arrival.

Section 27

Restraint of Trade

A contract that stops anyone from exercising a lawful profession, trade, or business is void. The "Non-Compete" Trap: You can't force an ex-employee to never work in your industry again. (Exception: Selling the "goodwill" of a business).

Section 30

Wagering (Gambling)

Agreements by way of wager are void. You cannot sue someone in an Indian court to recover money won in a bet. The law does not act as a debt collector for casinos.

Gambling vs. Insurance

Why is paying a bookie illegal, but paying an insurance company legal?

🎲

The Wager (Void)

"I bet you ₹10,000 your house will burn down."

Neither of us owns the house. We are just creating risk for entertainment. We have no Insurable Interest in the property.

🛡️

Insurance (Valid Contract)

"I pay you a premium, you pay me ₹10 Lakhs if MY house burns down."

I own the house. I suffer a real loss if it burns. The contract protects against an existing risk. This is a valid Contingent Contract (Sec 31).

Quasi-Contracts

Sections 68-72: Unjust Enrichment

Sometimes, there is no Offer, no Acceptance, and no Agreement. Yet, the law will invent a "fictional" contract to prevent someone from getting rich at the expense of another.

🍕

"Wrong House!"

"But it's delicious..."

Section 70

The Pizza Rule

A delivery boy accidentally drops off a paid premium pizza at your house instead of your neighbor's. You know you didn't order it, but you eat it anyway.

The Law Commands:

You enjoyed the benefit of a non-gratuitous act. You must compensate the restaurant for the pizza. A Quasi-Contract is formed by your action of eating it.

The Jurist's Library

The Precedents that shaped the law.

Trade (Sec 27)

Nordenfelt v. Maxim Nordenfelt

England (1894)

The Facts: Thorsten Nordenfelt was a famous inventor of guns. He sold his business to a company for a huge sum and signed a contract promising not to make guns anywhere in the world for 25 years. Later, he broke the promise and started making guns again.

The Jurist's Analysis:

Normally, stopping someone from trading is void. But the Court ruled this restraint was valid because it was "reasonable." He sold the worldwide "goodwill" of his business for massive money; if he started competing immediately, the buyers would have bought nothing. (This is the primary exception to Section 27).

Quasi (Sec 70)

State of West Bengal v. B.K. Mondal

Supreme Court of India (1962)

The Facts: A contractor built certain structures for the Government upon request from an officer. However, the strict legal formalities required to make a valid Government contract were not followed. The Govt used the buildings but refused to pay, saying "There is no valid contract!"

The Jurist's Analysis:

The Supreme Court invoked Section 70. Even though the formal contract was void, the contractor did the work lawfully, didn't intend to do it for free, and the Government enjoyed the benefit. The Government was ordered to pay compensation based on Quasi-Contract.

Docket 07

The Rival Software Dev

You hire a brilliant coder for your tech startup. Her employment contract states: "If you leave this company, you cannot work as a software developer for any other company in India for the next 5 years." She resigns and immediately joins a rival startup. You sue to stop her.

Legal Logic (Section 27): She wins. Any agreement that restrains someone from exercising a lawful profession is VOID. Indian courts are extremely strict about this. You cannot enforce a total ban on an employee's right to earn a livelihood after they leave your company. The clause is dead.

Docket 08

The Fire Rescue

Your neighbor's house catches on fire while they are on vacation. To stop the fire from spreading to your house, you hire a private water truck for ₹10,000 to extinguish the flames on their property. When the neighbor returns, you ask them to reimburse the ₹10,000. They refuse, saying "I never signed a contract asking you to do that."

Legal Logic (Section 70 & 69): You win. Under Quasi-Contract principles, if a person lawfully does anything for another person, not intending to do so gratuitously (for free), and the other person enjoys the benefit (their house was saved), they are bound to make compensation. The law invents a contract to ensure justice.

Professor's Module 05

The Specialized
Engagements.

"Beyond simple exchange lies the architecture of trust. Indemnity protects against ruin, Guarantees secure credit, Bailment guards property, and Agency extends human reach."

§124

The Shields of Commerce

Indemnity (Sec 124) vs. Guarantee (Sec 126)

Section 124

Indemnity

A contract where one party promises to save the other from loss caused to him by the conduct of the promisor himself, or by the conduct of any other person.

"If X sues you for selling me this car, I will pay your legal fees and any damages."

Did You Know?

In India, strictly speaking, Life and Fire Insurance do not fall under Section 124 (they are contingent contracts). But Marine Insurance and general indemnity bonds do.

Section 126

Guarantee

A contract to perform the promise, or discharge the liability, of a third person in case of his default. It involves three parties: Surety, Principal Debtor, and Creditor.

"Give A the loan. If A does not pay you back, I will."

Did You Know?

The modern global economy runs on Guarantees. "Letters of Credit" and "Bank Guarantees" are the mechanisms that allow international trade to happen when the buyer and seller don't trust each other.

Section 148

Bailment

The delivery of goods by one person to another for some purpose, upon a contract that they shall, when the purpose is accomplished, be returned or otherwise disposed of.

  • Delivery of Possession (not ownership).
  • Must be for a specific purpose.
  • Return of specific goods is mandatory.
🚗

Valet Parking

You give the keys; they hold possession.

👔

Dry Cleaning

Goods delivered for a purpose, then returned.

🏦

Bank Locker

NOT BAILMENT

Why isn't a bank locker bailment? Because you hold the key. The bank does not have exclusive possession of your jewelry. (Established in Atul Mehra v. Bank of Maharashtra).

The Law of Agency

Section 182: Extending the Self

Qui facit per alium, facit per se.

"He who acts through another does the act himself."

An Agent is a person employed to do any act for another, or to represent another in dealings with third persons. The person for whom such act is done is the Principal.

The Golden Rule (Sec 226)

Contracts entered into through an agent have the same legal consequences as if the Principal had entered into them directly.

Principal
Agent
3rd Party

The Agent connects the Principal to the Third Party, creating a direct contract between them.

Docket 09

The Stolen Tailor

You give expensive silk cloth to a tailor to stitch a suit. That night, thieves break into the tailor's locked shop and steal everything, including your silk. You sue the tailor to pay for the lost cloth. The tailor proves he locked the shop securely.

Legal Logic (Section 151 & 152): In a contract of bailment, the bailee (tailor) is bound to take as much care of the goods as a man of ordinary prudence would take of his own goods. If he proves he took that standard care (e.g., locked the shop), he is not responsible for the loss caused by theft or Act of God.

Docket 10

The Unauthorized Agent

"A" works as a purchasing agent for "P". P tells A, "Buy 50 laptops, but do not pay more than ₹50,000 each." A finds laptops for ₹55,000 each and buys them anyway on P's behalf. P refuses to pay the supplier, saying A exceeded his authority. The supplier sues P.

Legal Logic (Section 237 & Apparent Authority): The Supplier wins. If an agent does an act in the usual course of business, the Principal is bound by it even if the agent exceeded secret instructions, provided the third party did not know about the secret limit. P must pay the supplier, but P can separately sue his agent A for disobeying orders.

Professor's Module 06

The Tale of the
Midnight Bakery.

"To truly understand the law, we must see it breathe. Let us weave every section of the Contract Act into a single story of ambition, disaster, and justice."

§1-238
Chapter I: Formation
🤝

The Handshake

Arjun opens the 'Midnight Bakery'. He emails Ria, a coffee supplier: "I will buy 100 kg of premium coffee beans for ₹50,000." (The Offer - Sec 2a). Ria replies, "I agree." (The Acceptance - Sec 2b).

Both are over 18 and of sound mind (Capacity - Sec 11). The coffee and the ₹50,000 act as the mutually beneficial price for the promise (Consideration - Sec 2d). A legally binding contract is born!

Chapter II: Vitiating Factors
🎭

The Shadows of Deceit

A rival cafe owner threatens Ria: "Cancel your deal with Arjun, or I'll burn your warehouse." If Ria cancels out of fear, her consent is tainted by Coercion (Sec 15).

Alternatively, what if Ria deliberately mixes cheap tea powder into the premium coffee to trick Arjun? That is intentional deception—Fraud (Sec 17). Because of this deceit, Arjun has the power to cancel the contract. It is Voidable.

Chapter III: Frustration & Breach
⛈️

The Storm & The Betrayal

Ria packs the coffee, but a massive cyclone washes away the entire highway. Delivery is physically impossible. The law forgives her under the Doctrine of Frustration (Sec 56).

But what if there is no cyclone? What if Ria simply sells the coffee to someone else for ₹70,000? That is a Breach of Contract. Arjun loses his biggest wedding catering order because he had no coffee. Arjun sues Ria for Damages (Sec 73) to cover his lost profits.

Chapter IV: Special Contracts
🛡️

The Web of Trust

To survive the loss, Arjun takes a bank loan. The bank demands security. Arjun's wealthy friend, Kabir, steps in and says, "Give him the loan. If he defaults, I will pay." Kabir has created a Contract of Guarantee (Sec 126). He is the Surety.

To expand, Arjun gives his delivery van to a mechanic for repairs. The mechanic holds the van strictly for that purpose. This is a Bailment (Sec 148). Finally, Arjun hires an Agent (Sec 182) to negotiate coffee prices for him worldwide.

Professor's Module 07

The Midnight Bakery:
The Expansion.

"Our story is not quite over. As Arjun expands his business, he encounters the law of Contingencies, the rules of Pledging assets, and the art of rewriting a contract."

§31-172
Chapter V: The "If" Clause
🎲

The Contingent Contract

Arjun wants to buy a massive industrial oven from Germany. He tells the seller: "I will buy this oven for ₹5 Lakhs, IF my ship carrying my coffee exports safely arrives in London next month."

This is a Contingent Contract (Sec 31). The performance of the contract depends on an uncertain future event. If the ship sinks, the contract becomes void, and Arjun doesn't have to buy the oven. It is entirely legal (unlike a wager) because Arjun has a real business interest in the ship.

Chapter VI: The Security
💎

The Golden Pledge

While waiting for his shipment, Arjun runs out of cash to pay his staff. He goes to a moneylender and hands over his grandfather's vintage Rolex watch as security for a quick ₹50,000 loan.

This specific type of bailment—keeping goods as security for a debt—is called a Pledge (Sec 172). Arjun is the Pawnor; the moneylender is the Pawnee. If Arjun defaults, the moneylender has the right to sell the watch after giving reasonable notice.

Chapter VII: The Debts
🧾

Appropriation of Payments

Arjun owes his flour supplier three separate bills: ₹10k for January, ₹20k for February, and ₹30k for March. Arjun sends a cheque for ₹15k but forgets to say which bill it is for.

Under Section 59-61 (Clayton's Rule), if the debtor doesn't specify, the creditor can apply it to any lawful debt. Usually, the law applies it to discharge the debts in order of time (clearing January's bill first).

Chapter VIII: The Clean Slate
📜

Novation & Remission

Arjun owes the flour supplier a total of ₹60,000, but his business takes a hit. He meets the supplier and says, "I can't pay ₹60k. Will you accept a final settlement of ₹40k right now, and we tear up the old contract?" The supplier agrees.

This is perfectly legal! Section 62 (Novation) allows parties to substitute an old contract with a new one. Furthermore, Section 63 (Remission) allows a promisee to forgive or accept a lesser sum than what was owed. The old debt vanishes.

The Time Capsule

Did you know?

The Missing Chapters

If you read the bare act, you will notice Chapters VII and XI are missing! Why? In 1930 and 1932, the laws for selling goods and business partnerships became too big, so those chapters were "cut out" to create the Sale of Goods Act and the Partnership Act.

1872 meets 2026

This Act was written by the British over 150 years ago. Yet, when you click "I Agree" on a software Terms & Conditions pop-up today, you are being governed by the exact same 1872 principles of Offer and Acceptance (aided by the IT Act, 2000). Good logic never ages.

The Mastermind

The original draft was prepared by the Third Indian Law Commission, but the final version we read today was heavily influenced by Sir James Fitzjames Stephen. His focus was to make the law so simple that ordinary Indian merchants could understand it without lawyers.

Complex Law, Simplified.

Mastering the most confusing doctrines.

1. The "Stranger" Rule (Privity)

Privity of Contract vs. Privity of Consideration

The Confusion: Can a stranger sue?
The Simple Truth: Imagine A and B make a contract that A will pay C ₹10,000.

Rule 1 (Stranger to Contract): C cannot sue A because C is not a party to the contract. Only B can sue A.
Rule 2 (Stranger to Consideration): However, in India (unlike England), consideration can move from a third party! If C pays A the money on behalf of B, the contract between A and B is perfectly valid. (Established in Chinnaya v. Ramayya).

2. Void vs. Illegal Agreements

The "Poison" Effect

The Confusion: Aren't they the same thing? No!
The Simple Truth:

  • VOID = Dead. If you and I make a wagering agreement (a bet), it is void. If I borrow ₹10,000 from a friend to pay you the bet, my friend CAN recover the loan from me. The loan is valid.
  • ILLEGAL = Dead + Poisonous. If you and I agree to smuggle gold, it is illegal. If I borrow ₹10,000 from a friend to fund the smuggling (and he knows about it), the loan contract is also poisoned and dies. My friend CANNOT recover his money.

3. Quantum Meruit

"As much as is earned"

The Confusion: What happens if a contract is canceled halfway through? Do I get paid nothing?
The Simple Truth: The law hates unfairness. If you hire me to paint your 10-room house, and after I paint 5 rooms, you wrongfully fire me and cancel the contract. I cannot sue for the full contract price because I didn't finish. BUT, under the doctrine of Quantum Meruit, I can sue you for the value of the 5 rooms I already painted. You must pay for "as much as is earned."
